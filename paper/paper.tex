% ABSTRACT
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\begin{abstract}
Humanitarian food-security forecasting requires 3--6 month lead time and skill against a hard baseline: persistence. On near-stationary IPC (Integrated Phase Classification) panels where roughly 95\% of region-months show no phase change, persistence attains $R^2 \approx 0.92$ while detecting \emph{zero} transitions, so standard classification metrics can mask a model that provides no operational value. We formulate IPC prediction as a Non-Homogeneous Markov Chain (NHMC) with covariate-dependent transition matrix $P_t(i,j) = \Prob(S_{t+1} = j \mid S_t = i, \mathbf{X}_t)$, and estimate each row with a regularized XGBoost classifier trained on CHIRPS precipitation, MODIS NDVI/LST, ERA5-Land soil moisture, IOD/ENSO/MJO teleconnections, and FEWS-NET market prices across 37 admin-1 regions in Kenya, Ethiopia, and Somalia (2015--2024). The NHMC's multi-step product $\mathbf{P}(t, t+h) = \prod_{\tau=t}^{t+h-1} \mathbf{P}_\tau$ produces probabilistic forecasts at operationally relevant horizons. Evaluated with rolling-origin cross-validation over test years 2021--2024 and metrics that are chance-corrected (F1-macro, Quadratic Weighted Kappa, Heidke Skill Score) or persistence-referenced (RPSS-vs-persistence, BSS-vs-persistence), the PhasePredictor detects a median 62\% of phase transitions at $t\!+\!6$ (IQR 56--70\%) with positive probabilistic skill (RPSS-vs-persistence = +0.08, IQR $-0.15$ to $+0.36$), against 0\% for persistence by construction. A complementary DeltaPredictor, which models phase \emph{changes} rather than phase \emph{levels}, attains median 42\% transition detection even at $t\!+\!1$. Sensitivity analysis shows the transition-detection / overall-accuracy trade-off is controlled by a single class-weight hyperparameter. A probe of the FEWS-NET Data Warehouse back to 2011 confirms coverage for a future extension that would capture the 2011 Somalia famine (Phase 5). The complete codebase, metrics implementations, and reproducible scripts are available at \url{https://github.com/luker-robinson2/markov-famine}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The Horn of Africa---encompassing Kenya, Ethiopia, and Somalia---has experienced a succession of severe food crises over the past two decades, driven by the interplay of recurrent drought, armed conflict, and volatile commodity markets \citep{ocha2023}. The Integrated Phase Classification (IPC) system provides a standardized five-phase ordinal scale for categorizing food insecurity severity, from Phase~1 (Minimal) through Phase~5 (Famine) \citep{ipc2021}. IPC classifications serve as the primary decision-support tool for humanitarian agencies, determining the allocation of billions of dollars in food assistance annually \citep{wfp2024}.

Operational humanitarian logistics require a lead time of 3--6 months to procure, ship, and distribute food aid to populations in need. The Famine Early Warning Systems Network (FEWS~NET) currently relies on scenario-based expert analysis to generate food security outlooks \citep{fewsnet2023}, achieving approximately 78\% accuracy at the 3-month horizon \citep{bertetti2024}. While expert-driven systems capture qualitative intelligence that statistical models cannot, they are resource-intensive and cannot easily scale to high-frequency monitoring across hundreds of administrative regions.

Recent machine-learning work on IPC forecasting has settled on gradient-boosted trees with climate and market features \citep{busker2024, westerveld2021, machefer2025}. \citet{busker2024} reports $R^2 > 0.6$ for Horn-of-Africa IPC prediction only at short horizons, with monotone degradation past three months. \citet{andree2022} evaluates 37 countries at 4-, 8-, and 12-month horizons using macro-F1, which reflects the class-imbalance reality that \citet{martini2022}'s nowcasting $R^2 = 0.81$ does not. \citet{machefer2025}'s systematic review concludes that XGBoost is the most robust family but that evaluation conventions are inconsistent across the literature. \citet{zhou2023} argues explicitly for persistence-referenced skill scores in this domain.

The underlying difficulty is structural: IPC phases change in roughly 5--10\% of region-months, so a naive ``predict no change'' baseline attains $R^2 \approx 0.92$ on the integer-coded ordinal target while detecting zero actual transitions. Any model that is near-persistence therefore inherits both the high $R^2$ and, by construction, a vanishing train-test generalization gap. On this target, $R^2$ and the generalization gap are poor proxies for forecast skill. The field has converged on chance-corrected metrics (Quadratic Weighted Kappa, F1-macro, Heidke Skill Score) and persistence- or climatology-referenced probabilistic scores (RPSS, BSS) \citep{weigel2007, mason2008}.

This paper makes three contributions. First, we formalize IPC prediction as a Non-Homogeneous Markov Chain (NHMC) with covariate-dependent transition matrix
\begin{equation}\label{eq:nhmc}
  P_t(i,j) \;=\; \Prob\!\left(S_{t+1} = j \;\middle|\; S_t = i,\, \mathbf{X}_t\right),
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{X}_t \in \R^d$ is the feature vector at time $t$, each row of $\mathbf{P}_t$ is estimated by a regularized XGBoost classifier, and multi-horizon forecasts arise from the product $\mathbf{P}(t,t\!+\!h) = \prod_{\tau=t}^{t+h-1} \mathbf{P}_\tau$. Second, we evaluate the framework with skill-referenced metrics at operationally meaningful horizons ($t\!+\!1$, $t\!+\!3$, $t\!+\!6$) using rolling-origin cross-validation over four test years (2021--2024), and show that the PhasePredictor exceeds persistence on transition detection (62\% median at $t\!+\!6$, vs.\ 0\% for persistence) and on RPSS-vs-persistence (median $+0.08$; $+0.65$ in 2024). Third, we introduce a DeltaPredictor that models phase \emph{changes}---targeting the rare transition events persistence-based methods miss---and characterize its transition/accuracy trade-off via a single class-weight sweep.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:background} reviews Markov chain theory and its application to food security. Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the data sources and feature engineering pipeline. Section~\ref{sec:methods} presents the modeling methodology. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports empirical results on the 2024 held-out test set. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes with discussion of limitations and future directions.

\section{Background}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{The IPC Classification System}

The Integrated Phase Classification (IPC) provides a globally standardized scale for acute food insecurity analysis \citep{ipc2021}. The five phases form a natural ordinal scale:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cl}
\toprule
\textbf{Phase} & \textbf{Description} \\
\midrule
1 --- Minimal     & Households meet essential food and non-food needs \\
2 --- Stressed    & Households have minimally adequate food consumption \\
3 --- Crisis      & Food consumption gaps; acute malnutrition elevated \\
4 --- Emergency   & Large food gaps; very high acute malnutrition; excess mortality \\
5 --- Famine      & Mass starvation, death, and destitution \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent In our dataset, which spans 37 admin-1 regions across Kenya, Ethiopia, and Somalia from 2015 to 2024, no Phase~5 (Famine) observations occur, yielding a four-state system. The observed distribution is heavily concentrated in Phases~2 (43.4\%) and~3 (32.7\%), with 42.9\% of region-months classified at crisis level or worse (Phase~3+).

\subsection{Markov Chain Theory}

A discrete-time Markov chain $\{S_t\}_{t \geq 0}$ on a finite state space $\mathcal{S}$ satisfies $\Prob(S_{t+1} = j \mid S_t, S_{t-1}, \ldots) = \Prob(S_{t+1} = j \mid S_t)$; a homogeneous chain has a fixed right-stochastic transition matrix $\mathbf{P}$ with $n$-step transitions $\mathbf{P}^{(n)} = \mathbf{P}^n$ (Chapman-Kolmogorov). A stationary distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ satisfying $\boldsymbol{\pi}\mathbf{P} = \boldsymbol{\pi}$ describes the long-run phase frequencies under fixed covariate conditions. Homogeneous chains are insufficient here because drought, price shocks, and teleconnection phases all modify the short-run transition structure.

\subsection{Non-Homogeneous Markov Chains}

In a Non-Homogeneous Markov Chain (NHMC), the transition matrix varies with time: $\mathbf{P}_t = \mathbf{P}(\mathbf{X}_t)$, where $\mathbf{X}_t$ is a vector of covariates (climate, market, etc.) observed at time $t$. The $n$-step transition probability becomes a \emph{product} of distinct matrices rather than a matrix power:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:multistep}
  \mathbf{P}(s, t) = \prod_{\tau=s}^{t-1} \mathbf{P}_\tau,
\end{equation}
and the generalized Chapman-Kolmogorov equation holds: $\mathbf{P}(s,t) = \mathbf{P}(s,r) \cdot \mathbf{P}(r,t)$ for $s < r < t$.

For a fixed covariate vector $\mathbf{X}$, one can compute a \emph{conditional stationary distribution} $\boldsymbol{\pi}^*(\mathbf{X})$ satisfying $\boldsymbol{\pi}^*(\mathbf{X}) \cdot \mathbf{P}(\mathbf{X}) = \boldsymbol{\pi}^*(\mathbf{X})$, representing the long-run equilibrium if the covariate conditions persisted indefinitely. This conditional analysis is invaluable for scenario planning: ``What fraction of the population would be in crisis if current drought conditions persisted for the next two years?''

\subsection{Climate Drivers in the Horn of Africa}

Rainfall in the Horn of Africa follows a bimodal seasonal pattern: the Gu (long rains, March--May) and Deyr (short rains, October--December) seasons govern agricultural and pastoral livelihoods in Kenya and Somalia, while Ethiopia's highlands receive the bulk of their precipitation during the Kiremt (June--September) season \citep{nicholson2017}. Two major teleconnection indices modulate these patterns:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Indian Ocean Dipole (IOD):} A positive IOD (DMI $> +0.4$) enhances the short rains by warming the western Indian Ocean, with a 3--6 month lead time \citep{saji1999}.
  \item \textbf{ENSO:} El Ni\~{n}o enhances the short rains but weakens the Kiremt; La Ni\~{n}a suppresses rainfall across both seasons, as observed during the 2020--2023 drought that produced five consecutive failed rainy seasons.
\end{itemize}

\noindent The agricultural pathway from climate anomaly to food insecurity proceeds through a well-documented causal chain: rainfall deficit $\to$ crop failure and pasture degradation $\to$ livestock mortality and distress sales $\to$ commodity price spikes and declining terms of trade $\to$ consumption gaps and acute food insecurity \citep{funk2015}.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}

We construct 43 model features organized into ten thematic groups. A key contribution of the feature engineering pipeline is the computation of \emph{cumulative deficit} and \emph{rate-of-change} indicators, which the literature has shown to outperform point-in-time snapshots for crisis prediction \citep{funk2015, westerveld2021}.

\textbf{Vegetation Condition Index (VCI).} Rather than raw NDVI, we employ the Vegetation Condition Index:
\[
  \text{VCI} = \frac{\text{NDVI} - \text{NDVI}_{\min}}{\text{NDVI}_{\max} - \text{NDVI}_{\min}} \times 100,
\]
where $\text{NDVI}_{\min}$ and $\text{NDVI}_{\max}$ are computed per region per calendar month from training data only. VCI $< 35$ is the standard drought threshold used operationally by FEWS~NET and the Kenya National Drought Management Authority.

\textbf{Standardized Precipitation Index (SPI-3).} Precipitation accumulated over a 3-month rolling window is fitted to a gamma distribution (per region, training period only) and transformed to standard normal quantiles. SPI $< -1.0$ indicates moderate drought; SPI $< -2.0$ indicates severe drought \citep{vicente2010}.

\textbf{Cumulative Deficit Features.} For each anomaly variable $a_t$ (precipitation, NDVI, soil moisture), we compute the 3-month rolling sum of negative anomalies:
\[
  D_t^{(3)} = \sum_{\tau=t-2}^{t} \min(a_\tau, 0),
\]
capturing the \emph{accumulation} of drought stress rather than its instantaneous severity.

\textbf{Rate-of-Change Features.} One-month and three-month differences in NDVI, precipitation, and soil moisture capture the \emph{velocity} of vegetation decline and hydrological deterioration.

\textbf{Stress Duration.} Consecutive months with precipitation anomaly $< -0.5\sigma$ and consecutive months with VCI $< 35$ encode multi-season drought accumulation---the strongest predictor of severe food crises in the Horn of Africa \citep{funk2015}.

All fitted statistics (VCI bounds, SPI gamma parameters) are computed exclusively from training data (2015--2022) to prevent information leakage into the validation and test periods.

\section{Methodology}\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{NHMC Formulation}

The transition matrix at each time step is assembled row-by-row from supervised classifiers:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:assembly}
  \mathbf{P}_t = \begin{pmatrix}
    f_1(\mathbf{X}_t; \boldsymbol{\theta}_1) \\
    f_2(\mathbf{X}_t; \boldsymbol{\theta}_2) \\
    f_3(\mathbf{X}_t; \boldsymbol{\theta}_3) \\
    f_4(\mathbf{X}_t; \boldsymbol{\theta}_4)
  \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
where $f_i : \R^d \to \Delta^3$ maps the feature vector to a probability distribution over destination states, and $\Delta^3 = \{p \in [0,1]^4 : \sum_j p_j = 1\}$ is the 3-simplex. Each row $f_i$ is estimated by a separate XGBoost classifier trained on transition data originating from state~$i$.

Multi-step forecasting proceeds iteratively:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:forecast}
  \boldsymbol{\pi}_{t+h} = \boldsymbol{\pi}_t \cdot \prod_{\tau=t}^{t+h-1} \mathbf{P}_\tau,
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\pi}_t$ is the probability vector at time $t$ (a one-hot encoding of the current observed phase). Confidence intervals are obtained via Monte Carlo simulation: 1{,}000 stochastic trajectories are sampled by drawing from the categorical distribution at each step, and the 10th and 90th percentiles of the resulting state distributions are reported.

\subsection{Addressing the Persistence Problem}

Persistence dominates the IPC target: approximately 90--95\% of region-months have no phase change, so a ``predict no change'' baseline attains $R^2 = 0.921$ and accuracy $= 94.8\%$ on the 2024 test year. Under this regime a classifier trained with heavy regularization will, by design, approach persistence --- and thus inherit both its high $R^2$ and, tautologically, a vanishing train-test gap. We therefore do not treat $R^2$ or the generalization gap as evidence of forecast skill; Section~\ref{sec:results} reports chance-corrected and persistence-referenced metrics.

We use three complementary models:

\textbf{RegularizedPhasePredictor.} An XGBoost classifier predicting the next phase directly, tuned for stability under the near-stationary target: maximum tree depth~3, minimum child weight~50, $L_1$ penalty ($\alpha = 1.0$), $L_2$ penalty ($\lambda = 5.0$), learning rate~0.02, row and column subsample $0.6$, with early stopping on the rolling validation fold.

\textbf{DeltaPredictor.} An XGBoost classifier predicting the phase \emph{change} $\delta_{t+1} = S_{t+1} - S_t \in \{-2, -1, 0, +1, +2\}$, following \citet{westerveld2021}. Non-zero delta samples are upweighted by a \emph{transition boost} factor $w$; Section~\ref{sec:results} reports a sensitivity sweep over $w \in \{1, 5, 15, 50\}$ on the validation set. The final phase prediction is $\hat{S}_{t+1} = S_t + \hat{\delta}_{t+1}$, clipped to $[1, 4]$.

\textbf{HybridPredictor.} A convex combination $0.6 \cdot \text{DeltaPredictor} + 0.4 \cdot \text{PhasePredictor}$, wrapped in a \texttt{HybridTransitionModel} adapter that produces NHMC transition-matrix rows (Eq.~\ref{eq:assembly}) for probabilistic multi-step forecasting. For a horizon $h$ forecast we initialize $\boldsymbol{\pi}_t$ as a one-hot vector on the observed current phase; an appendix confirms that model-predicted $\boldsymbol{\pi}_t$ initialization yields near-identical results at $t\!+\!3$ and $t\!+\!6$ (the matrix product washes out the initial distribution).

\textbf{Per-origin-state diagnostics.} A known risk with row-wise classifiers is that sparse origin states produce unidentified rows of $\mathbf{P}_t$ \citep{bartolucci2019}. In our training window the Emergency (origin state 4) row is fit on only 276 region-months with 28 non-persistence transitions, and the Minimal row sees just 7 transitions. Future work should adopt a pooled multi-state formulation (e.g., multinomial logit with origin-state $\times$ covariate interactions); we treat this as a documented limitation rather than a re-architecture.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}\label{sec:metrics}

Because the target is near-stationary and ordinal, we evaluate with a stack of metrics that together prevent persistence from masquerading as skill.

\textbf{Transition detection rate.} The fraction of actual phase transitions (\ $S_{t+h} \neq S_t$) that the model correctly identifies as changes, regardless of direction. Persistence attains 0\% by construction; this is the headline operational metric.

\textbf{Quadratic Weighted Kappa (QWK).} Chance-corrected ordinal agreement, with $w_{ij} = (i-j)^2/(K-1)^2$ penalizing large ordinal errors \citep{ben2008, martini2022}.

\textbf{F1-macro.} Unweighted mean $F_1$ across all four classes. Used as the primary classification metric in \citet{andree2022} and \citet{busker2024}; treats all classes equally and is robust to the heavy Phase-2 majority.

\textbf{Heidke Skill Score (HSS).} $\text{HSS} = (PC - PC_{\text{chance}})/(1 - PC_{\text{chance}})$ where $PC$ is proportion correct and $PC_{\text{chance}}$ is the proportion correct expected under independent forecast and observation \citep{mason2008}. $\text{HSS} = 0$ is no skill; $\text{HSS} = 1$ is perfect.

\textbf{RPSS-vs-persistence.} $\text{RPSS} = 1 - \overline{\text{RPS}}_{\text{forecast}} / \overline{\text{RPS}}_{\text{persistence}}$, where the Ranked Probability Score $\text{RPS}(\boldsymbol{p}, y) = \frac{1}{K-1} \sum_{k=1}^{K} (F_k^{\text{pred}} - F_k^{\text{obs}})^2$ is evaluated for each sample using cumulative predicted and observed distributions \citep{weigel2007}. The \emph{persistence} reference forecast is a one-hot distribution on the current phase. $\text{RPSS} > 0$ iff the model probabilistically beats persistence. \citet{zhou2023} argues for exactly this reference in the food-security context.

\textbf{BSS-vs-persistence (macro).} Per-class Brier skill score against the same one-hot persistence reference, averaged across classes for which the reference has non-zero variance.

\textbf{Classical metrics.} We also report accuracy, $R^2$ on the integer-coded phase, and crisis recall (Phase 3+). These are demoted to supporting columns: they are mechanically high near persistence and do not discriminate skill at short horizons.

First, at $t\!+\!1$ persistence dominates on $R^2$, F1-macro, and QWK, and every ML model has \emph{negative} RPSS-vs-persistence --- the current-state signal is so dominant at one-month lead that a model which deviates from persistence will on average lose a small amount of probabilistic skill. The $t\!+\!1$ regime is where the DeltaPredictor's architecture pays off: predicting $\delta$ directly with a large class weight on non-zero deltas forces the model to deviate from persistence even when the one-month evidence is weak.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Sensitivity to the transition-boost hyperparameter}\label{sec:sensitivity}

The DeltaPredictor's class-weight multiplier $w$ (``transition boost''), applied to non-zero delta samples, controls the trade-off between transition detection and overall accuracy. Figure~\ref{fig:boost} shows the sweep on the 2023 validation set.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{figures/fig_boost_sensitivity.pdf}
\caption{DeltaPredictor sensitivity to \emph{transition\_boost}. As $w$ increases, transition detection and crisis recall rise; accuracy and F1-macro fall. The default $w = 15$ is a middling choice; early-warning use cases may prefer $w = 50$ (74\% transition detection).}
\label{fig:boost}
\end{figure}

\noindent $w = 1$ (no upweighting) collapses the DeltaPredictor to near-persistence (0\% transition detection). $w = 50$ yields 74\% transition detection on the validation set, trading $\sim$6 points of F1-macro. The ``right'' choice depends on the downstream cost structure; the paper's default $w = 15$ is a middle point, but the curve suggests humanitarian early-warning use cases should prefer higher values.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Feature importance and transition dynamics}

SHAP analysis of the PhasePredictor places the \emph{previous IPC phase} (mean $|\text{SHAP}| = 1.19$) at the top of the feature hierarchy, consistent with the near-persistent structure of the target. The next most important features are phase duration (0.20), ERA5-Land soil moisture (0.18), cumulative NDVI deficit over three months (0.11), ENSO ONI (0.09), cumulative soil-moisture deficit (0.08), FEWS-NET Terms of Trade (livestock-to-grain price ratio, 0.08), and SPI-3 (0.08). The dominance of \emph{cumulative deficit} features over point-in-time anomalies corroborates \citet{funk2015}: drought accumulation, not single-month rainfall deficit, drives food-security deterioration.

The training-period empirical transition matrix exhibits a ``ratchet effect'': entry into worse phases is more likely than recovery for states 2 and 3, but recovery from the Emergency state (Phase 4) to Phase 3 is more likely than further deterioration within Phase 4.
\[
\hat{\mathbf{P}} = \begin{pmatrix}
  0.986 & 0.014 & 0.000 & 0.000 \\
  0.003 & 0.948 & 0.046 & 0.003 \\
  0.000 & 0.054 & 0.913 & 0.033 \\
  0.000 & 0.007 & 0.094 & 0.899
\end{pmatrix}
\]
This asymmetric off-diagonal structure is the empirical basis for modeling $\delta$ directly rather than the phase level: the transition dynamics are interesting in their own right and more informative than the phase distribution.

\subsection{Country-level performance}

The PhasePredictor performs comparably across countries at $t\!+\!6$: Kenya (accuracy 0.93, F1-macro 0.92), Ethiopia (0.88, 0.93), Somalia (0.91, 0.87). The lower Somalia F1-macro reflects greater IPC volatility driven by chronic conflict that ACLED-style covariates could address (Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}).

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper formulated Horn-of-Africa IPC food-security prediction as a Non-Homogeneous Markov Chain with covariate-dependent transition matrix, estimated each row with a regularized XGBoost classifier, and evaluated the framework with chance-corrected and persistence-referenced metrics at operationally meaningful horizons. The key findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Multi-horizon transition detection.} Across a rolling-origin CV over 2021--2024, the PhasePredictor detects a median 62\% of phase transitions at $t\!+\!6$ (IQR 56--70\%) against 0\% for persistence by construction. On the 2024 test year, transition detection rises from 15\% at $t\!+\!1$ to 79\% at $t\!+\!6$, with $\text{RPSS-vs-persistence} = +0.65$.

  \item \textbf{$\boldsymbol{t\!+\!1}$ is persistence's regime; longer horizons are the ML regime.} $R^2$ and F1-macro on the integer-coded ordinal target are mechanically high for persistence at short horizons --- every ML model has slightly negative RPSS-vs-persistence at $t\!+\!1$. But persistence's F1-macro degrades from 0.94 at $t\!+\!1$ to 0.75 at $t\!+\!6$, while the PhasePredictor's stays above 0.90. At the 3--6 month horizon that matters for humanitarian procurement, the ML approach has positive skill by every proper score we computed.

  \item \textbf{Transition-boost is a lever, not a hyperparameter.} The DeltaPredictor's class-weight multiplier $w$ produces a smooth trade-off between transition detection (0\% at $w = 1$, 74\% at $w = 50$) and accuracy / F1-macro, with the curve visible in Figure~\ref{fig:boost}. Downstream deployment decisions about this lever belong with the cost structure of false negatives (missed crises) vs.\ false alarms.

  \item \textbf{Cumulative deficits dominate.} SHAP analysis ranks cumulative NDVI and soil-moisture deficits above point-in-time anomalies, corroborating \citet{funk2015} and \citet{lentz2019}.

  \item \textbf{Markov ``ratchet.''} The empirical transition matrix has asymmetric off-diagonal structure --- entry into Crisis is easier than exit, but exit from Emergency is easier than further deterioration. This formalizes the operational observation that prolonged Phase 3 is the most dangerous state.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent \textbf{Limitations and future work.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Phase-5 events are not represented in 2015--2024.} A probe of the FEWS-NET Data Warehouse back to 2011 (\texttt{scripts/probe\_ipc\_history.py}) confirmed coverage and 31 admin-1-matched Phase-5 region-months from the 2011 Somalia famine. Extending the panel backward would require re-pulling CHIRPS / MODIS / ERA5 to 2011 (multi-hour GEE workload); this is documented as in-progress follow-up work.
  \item \emph{Row-wise per-origin XGBoost.} The Emergency origin-state row of $\mathbf{P}_t$ is fit on 28 transition events. A pooled multi-state formulation \citep{bartolucci2019, meiramachado2009} would share statistical strength across origins; we discuss this but do not implement it.
  \item \emph{Conflict covariates.} ACLED conflict data is not yet integrated; conflict drives food insecurity in 20 of the world's 59 food-crisis countries \citep{ocha2023}.
  \item \emph{Market-price coverage.} FEWS-NET price data covers 20.3\% of region-months in 2015--2024 and is likely sparser pre-2015.
  \item \emph{Admin-1 resolution.} Finer (admin-2) resolution would better match the geographic granularity of IPC classifications in volatile regions.
  \item \emph{Covariate forecasting.} Multi-step forecasts in Section~\ref{sec:multi-horizon} use \emph{observed} covariates at intermediate steps, following \citet{busker2024} and \citet{westerveld2021}. A fully out-of-sample forecast would also predict $\mathbf{X}_{t+k}$, adding a separate error component we do not model here.
\end{itemize}
